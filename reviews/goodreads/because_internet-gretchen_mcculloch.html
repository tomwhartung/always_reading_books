Because Internet, Understanding the New Rules of Language is a book written by Gretchen McCulloch and published in 2019. The author is a linguist who has spent her life studying how the English language has changed through the ages, and she is excited about its recent rapid evolution. In Chapter 1, Informal Writing, she writes "You never truly step into the same English twice" [p.15]. I am giving Because Internet 5 of 5 stars because I found it to be very informative. Most importantly, reading the book has taught me the difference between formal and informal writing, granting me the freedom to loosen up when exchanging messages with friends — assuming I dare to do so. I for one like to obey all the rules, and feel like both of my late grandmothers will judge me harshly if I do not proofread everything and make it just exactly perfect. Plus: once you send it, it's too late to fix it, and it will be wrong forever! Ms. McCulloch sees things differently, and regards changes to the language as inevitable. In Chapter 2, Language and Society, she writes "every generation has talked slightly differently from its parents: otherwise, we'd all still be talking like Shakespeare" [p. 30]. She also claims linguists estimate "that women lead 90 percent of linguistic change" [p. 34], and that the internet "makes language change faster" [p. 39]. These things are good to know, and the book is full of tidbits like this. One of the things I like most about the book is her discussion of sarcasm. Gretchen writes that conveying irony in written material was a "problem long before the internet" [ p. 133]. She mentions how Henry Denham and John Wilkins proposed new punctuation symbols intended to communicate sarcasm centuries ago, in 1575 and 1668 respectively. Henry's and John's new punctuation marks failed to take hold, but that hasn't stopped people today from coming up with innovative ways to express irony and sarcasm online and in text messages.
If polite typography, as we saw earlier, is about making extra effort, using initial capitals and friendly exclamation marks to signal cheerful distance or genuine enthusiasm, then ironic [e.g., raNdoMly mIxeD caSe] typography is the opposite on both counts: it introduces a note of dissonance that makes the reader look harder to find the double meaning. Any variation from an expected baseline will do, whether that's lowercasing, sparkle sarcasm [e.g., "~~~***~~~sparkle sarcasm~~~***~~~"], asking a rhetorical question by omitting the question mark, or ironically using outdated slang [such as "gr8"].... — From Because Internet, by Gretchen McCulloch, p. 149.
Fun with case and sparkles aside, the simplest way to achieve "deadpan snark" McCulloch claims, is "often simply an initial ~tilde" [p. 139]. Another interesting observation Ms. McCulloch makes regards emoticons and emoji, and she dedicates all of Chapter 5, Emoji and Other Internet Gestures, to the topic. Some people might think these constitute a language unto themselves, but she refutes this notion. Instead she asserts they are more like the gestures we make when speaking. "Just like gestures and facial expressions fit seamlessly with spoken words, punctuation-based emoticons can directly accompany typed words" [p. 178]. The book is informative and McCulloch's attitude is positive. For example, near the end of Chapter 4, Typographical Tone of Voice, the author confesses "I, for one, think this change is fantastic." She says she would "gladly accept the decline of standards that were arbitrary and elitist in the first place in favor of being able to connect with my fellow humans" [p. 153]. I recommend Because Internet to anyone interested in learning how the internet is causing the English language to evolve. But only time will tell if I can take McCulloch's suggestions to heart and have some fun being more informal. Somehow I am convinced being loose with language will backfire somehow. Somehow I feel my grandmothers — or perhaps future generations — are watching, ready to judge my indiscretions, no matter how minor, private, deliberate, and even calculated. Either way, it's always fun to learn what the kids are up to these days, and I am glad I read the book!
